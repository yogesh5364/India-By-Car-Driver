About This Tour

Ladakh is not a destination — it is a state of mind. At an average altitude of 3,500 metres, this high-altitude desert in the trans-Himalayan region offers landscapes so alien and magnificent that photographs fail to capture them. In 9 days, this circuit takes you across the world's highest motorable passes including Khardung La (5,359m), into the dune-swept Nubra Valley with its Bactrian camels, to the electrifying blue of Pangong Lake straddling the India-China border, and through ancient Tibetan Buddhist monasteries that have clung to cliffsides for over a thousand years.

Our Ladakh-specialist drivers know these mountain roads intimately — the unpaved river-crossings, the altitude acclimatisation stops, the military checkpoint procedures, and the local guesthouses with the best warming thukpa. This is not a soft tour. It is an adventure. But you will be in safe, expert hands every kilometre of the way.

Day-by-Day Itinerary

01
Arrival in Leh — Acclimatisation
Day 1

Arrive at Kushok Bakula Rimpochhe Airport, Leh (3,524m). This day is mandatory rest — your body needs time to adjust to the altitude. Light walk around Leh Market and the old town in the evening. Dinner and early sleep. Do not attempt any strenuous activity on Day 1. Your driver will brief you on the journey ahead over chai.

Leh Airport Pickup Leh Market (Easy Walk) Acclimatisation Rest
02
Leh — Local Sightseeing
Day 2

Now properly acclimatised, explore Leh. Visit the 9-storey Leh Palace — a miniature Potala — perched above the old city. Walk the narrow lanes below, stop at the ancient Jama Masjid, and visit the Shanti Stupa offering panoramic Himalayan views. Evening at the Hall of Fame museum, dedicated to the Indian Army's feats in Ladakh. Check permits needed for further travel — your driver handles all paperwork.

Leh Palace Shanti Stupa Hall of Fame Museum
03
Leh — Monastery Circuit
Day 3 · ~80 km loop

Drive along the Indus Valley on a monastery circuit — Shey Palace and Monastery, the spectacular 12-storey Thiksey Monastery (the "Potala of Ladakh") with its towering Maitreya Buddha statue, and the cave monastery of Hemis — the largest and wealthiest in Ladakh, home to a famous annual festival. Return to Leh via Stakna and Matho monasteries. This is one of the finest Buddhist heritage drives in the world.

Thiksey Monastery Hemis Monastery Shey Palace
04
Leh → Nubra Valley via Khardung La
Day 4 · ~120 km

The drive everyone dreams of. Ascend to Khardung La Pass at 5,359 metres — one of the highest motorable roads on Earth. The views are staggering: glaciers on one side, desert valleys on the other. Descend into Nubra Valley — a high-altitude desert shaped by the Shyok and Nubra rivers, flanked by snow peaks. Check into a camp or guesthouse at Hunder or Diskit.

Khardung La Pass (5,359m) Diskit Monastery Hunder Sand Dunes
05
Nubra Valley — Camel Safari & Turtuk
Day 5

Morning camel safari on the Hunder sand dunes riding Bactrian double-humped camels — a surreal experience with the Himalayas as a backdrop. Drive further north to Turtuk — the last village open to civilians before the Pakistan border, home to the Balti people with their unique culture, apricot orchards, and stone houses. One of the most remote and beautiful villages in India. Return to Nubra for the night.

Bactrian Camel Safari Turtuk Village Shyok River Banks
06
Nubra → Pangong Lake
Day 6 · ~160 km

The most scenic drive of the entire trip. Travel via the Shyok Valley to Pangong Tso (4,350m) — the lake made famous by the film 3 Idiots but truly beyond any cinema. The colour shifts from turquoise to cobalt to deep sapphire depending on the light and hour. The lake extends 134 km, with 60% in Tibet. Arrive for sunset when the water turns molten gold. Stay in a lakeside camp.

Shyok Valley Drive Pangong Lake Sunset Lakeside Camp Stay
07
Pangong — Sunrise & Return to Leh
Day 7 · ~220 km

Wake before dawn for the Pangong sunrise — an experience of extraordinary beauty as the first light turns the lake pink and orange. Spend the morning at the lake, then drive back to Leh via the Chang La Pass (5,360m) and Druk White Lotus School. Stop at Rancho's School — the actual filming location from 3 Idiots — and the mesmerising Magnetic Hill. Arrive in Leh by evening.

Pangong Sunrise Chang La Pass (5,360m) Magnetic Hill
08
Leh — Confluence & Alchi
Day 8 · ~70 km loop

The famous Sangam — confluence of the turquoise Zanskar and blue-grey Indus rivers — is a photographer's dream, best seen by a short coracle (round boat) ride. Continue to Alchi Monastery, a 10th-century marvel with the finest Buddhist murals in the Indian Himalayas. Visit Nimmu and Basgo Palace ruins on the return. Farewell dinner in Leh — try the local thukpa, momos, and butter tea.

Zanskar–Indus Confluence Alchi Monastery Basgo Palace
09
Leh — Departure
Day 9

Early morning drop to Kushok Bakula Rimpochhe Airport for your flight out. Last views of the Stok Kangri massif as you climb away from this extraordinary land. Most travellers say Ladakh ruined them for ordinary travel — you will understand why. A Manali road exit (2-day drive via Rohtang and Baralacha La) can be arranged as an alternative departure for the adventurous.

Leh Airport Drop Optional: Manali Exit Route

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best time to visit Ladakh?
June to September is the Ladakh travel window — the only months when all high mountain passes including Khardung La, Chang La, and the Manali–Leh Highway are fully open. July–August is peak season with the best weather and road conditions. Late September sees the first snowfall at higher passes. Pangong Lake can be visited year-round by flying to Leh, but Nubra via Khardung La is June–September only.
Will I get altitude sickness? How do I prepare?
Altitude sickness (AMS) is a genuine risk — Leh sits at 3,524m and passes exceed 5,000m. We build a mandatory 2-day acclimatisation into the itinerary. Drink 3–4 litres of water daily, avoid alcohol on Days 1–2, take light walks only. Consult your doctor about Diamox (acetazolamide) before travel — many travellers use it preventively. Symptoms of AMS include headache, nausea, and breathlessness; inform your driver immediately if you feel unwell.
Do I need special permits for Ladakh?
Yes. Indian nationals require an Inner Line Permit (ILP) for Nubra Valley, Pangong Lake, and Hanle. Foreign nationals require a Protected Area Permit (PAP) for most areas beyond Leh. Our driver handles all permit applications — you just need to provide your ID/passport details in advance. Permits are processed in Leh before departure. This is included in our service; no extra paperwork stress for you.
What type of vehicle is used for Ladakh roads?
We use Innova Crysta or Tata Safari for Ladakh circuits — both are high ground-clearance vehicles well-suited to mountain roads and river crossings. Our Ladakh drivers are experienced specifically with these mountain conditions, including rocky BRO roads, river fords, and high-altitude cold starts. We do not use regular sedans for Ladakh terrain.
Can the trip be extended to the Manali–Leh Highway?
Yes — the Manali–Leh Highway (470 km, 2 days) via Rohtang Pass, Baralacha La, Lachulung La, and Tanglang La is one of the greatest road journeys on Earth. You can either start from Manali and fly out of Leh, or fly into Leh and drive out to Manali. This adds 2–3 days and is best done June–September when the highway is open.
